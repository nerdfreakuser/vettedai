<h2>Buying Guide: How to Choose a 3D Printer in 2026</h2></p>
<p>When deciding which 3D printer is right for you, consider these three main factors:</p>
<h3>1. FDM vs. Resin</h3>
<ul class="list-disc pl-6 mb-4"><li class="list-disc"><strong>FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling):</strong> These printers melt spools of plastic. They are best for practical parts, cosplay, toys, and general hobby use. FDM printers are generally safer, less messy, and easier to live with indoors.</li>
<li class="list-disc"><strong>Resin (SLA/MSLA):</strong> These printers use UV light to cure liquid resin. They are best for incredibly detailed, small objects like tabletop miniatures. However, the resin is toxic, requires gloves/masks, and needs alcohol baths for cleaning.</li>
</ul>
<h3>2. Speed and Mechanics (CoreXY vs. Bed Slingers)</h3>
The biggest shift in the market recently is the move towards <strong>CoreXY</strong> machines (like the Bambu P1S and Creality K1C). These printers move the print head quickly along the X and Y axes while the bed slowly drops, allowing for incredibly fast print speeds (up to 500mm/s). Traditional <strong>Bed Slingers</strong> (like the Anycubic Kobra 3 Max) move the heavy build plate back and forth. They are generally cheaper and can scale larger more easily, but are limited in speed.
<h3>3. Enclosed vs. Open Frame</h3>
If you only plan to print PLA and PETG, an open-frame printer is perfectly fine. But if you want to print engineering materials like ABS, ASA, or Nylon—which require high, stable ambient temperatures to prevent warping—you absolutely need a fully enclosed printer like the Bambu Lab P1S or Creality K1C.
